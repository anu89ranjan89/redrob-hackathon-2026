Job Description: Senior Product Manager — Core Payments (Founding Product Team)

Company: FinFlow Technologies (Series B FinTech Infrastructure Platform)

Location: Bengaluru/Hyderabad, India (Hybrid)

Employment Type: Full-time

Experience Required: 5–10 years (see "what we mean by this" below)

Let's be honest about this role

Most Product Manager JDs read like someone copy-pasted a list of buzzwords from LinkedIn.

We're going to try something different.

We are building infrastructure that moves money between businesses, banks, payment gateways, and financial institutions. The product is complicated. The stakeholders are complicated. The regulations are complicated.

The role is not.

We need someone who can figure out what matters and ignore what doesn't.

If your idea of product management is spending weeks polishing PRDs while engineers wait for decisions, this isn't the role.

If your idea of product management is shipping features without understanding customer pain points, this also isn't the role.

We need someone who can move comfortably between:

Deep customer understanding.
Ruthless execution.

The best PMs we've worked with don't see those as separate skills.

What you'd actually be doing

The high-level mandate:

Own the end-to-end experience of our payments infrastructure products.

That means understanding merchant pain points, prioritizing roadmap decisions, defining product strategy, and working with engineering teams to ship solutions.

In practical terms, your first 90 days will probably look like:

Weeks 1–3

Understand our products, customers, and operational bottlenecks.

Talk to merchants.

Talk to support teams.

Talk to engineers.

Figure out where the real problems are.

Weeks 4–8

Define and launch at least one high-impact product initiative.

Not a roadmap.

Not a presentation.

A shipped product improvement.

Weeks 9–12

Build a measurable framework for product success.

Customer retention.

Feature adoption.

Transaction success rates.

Revenue impact.

Whatever matters most.

Your job is making sure we stop arguing about opinions and start looking at evidence.

What we mean by "5–10 years"

The number is approximate.

We've hired excellent PMs with 4 years of experience and rejected PMs with 15.

We're evaluating judgment, not age.

That said, here are the disqualifiers we actually apply:

If you've only worked on internal tools

We will probably not move forward.

This role requires understanding external customer behavior.

If you've spent most of your career writing documents but rarely shipping products

We will probably not move forward.

We care about outcomes.

If your experience is primarily project management rather than product management

We will probably not move forward.

Managing timelines is important.

It's not the job.

How to read between the lines

The ideal candidate we're imagining is roughly:

6–8 years of product management experience.
Has owned a customer-facing product from conception to scale.
Strong analytical mindset.
Comfortable making decisions with incomplete information.
Has worked closely with engineering teams.
Understands business tradeoffs, not just product tradeoffs.
Located in or willing to relocate to Bengaluru or Hyderabad.
Actively exploring opportunities and likely to engage with recruiters.

We know this is a narrow profile.

That's intentional.

We'd rather interview 20 strong candidates than 500 weak matches.

Final note for the participants of the Candidate Discovery & Ranking Challenge

The wrong answer to this JD is finding candidates whose profiles contain the most product-management keywords.

That's a trap.

The right answer is understanding what the role actually requires.

A candidate who has never used the phrase "product strategy" but has successfully launched and scaled customer-facing products may be an excellent fit.

A candidate with every PM buzzword imaginable but no evidence of ownership, customer interaction, or measurable impact may not be.

Your ranking system should also account for behavioral signals.

A perfect-on-paper candidate who hasn't logged in for months and never responds to recruiters is less valuable than a slightly weaker candidate who is actively engaged and open to opportunities.

For hiring purposes, availability matters.
